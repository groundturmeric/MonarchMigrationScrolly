Monarch Migration

a symbol resilience

The annual migration of millions of Monarch butterflies across central and eastern U.S. and southern Canada is one of the most fascinating phenomena we can still witness today.

However, over the last few years, this fragile living wonder has been in affected by various human activities such as agricultural farming changing grasslands into farms, the use of herbicides, deforestation, all of which also contribute to the loss of native milkweed plants, an essential host plant for the Monarch.

Despite all these challenges, the Monarch butterflies successfully complete a most epic annual cycle of migration accomplished in about 4 generations!

Monarch Community Science Program

Finding data from this epic migration is very challenging since Monarch Butterflies are not easy to track. I was excited to come across this crowd sourced data set from “Journey North” where users contribute by reporting sightings, this geo located data adds a new lens to observe these migrations throughout the seasons.

Interactive Map of Annual Monarch Migrations

Generation 1

March

Generation 1 Monarch eggs are commonly laid on species of milkweed from late March through April in the southern United States and northern Mexico.

April

It may take Generation 1 Larvae up to up to 45 days to develop from eggs to adults ready to start flying north.

May

Generation 1 Adults mate and begin to lay eggs about four days after emerging, and continue the journey north, laying eggs along the way. They begin to arrive in the northern US and southern Canada in late May.

Generation 2

June

Monarchs in Generation 2 are laid throughout much of eastern North America during the month of June. Generation 2 larvae also eat milkweed species.

July

Generation 2 Adults emerge in July they fly north as the southern summers can get too hot and dry. They mate, and lay eggs (Generation 3). Some Generation 3 Monarchs emerge early enough to reproduce in the northern part of their breeding range.

Generation 3 and 4

August

Generation 3 individuals that emerge late in August will undergo diapause in order to migrate south to the overwintering sites in Mexico, with most Generation 4 individuals.
Diapause is a delayed maturity; these young Adults do not reproduce right after they emerge. as temperatures decrease and daylengths shorten at the end of the summer, instead of mating and laying eggs, they spend their time drinking nectar and clustering together in nighttime roosts in preparation for their long journey south.

September

The same Generation flies from their summer habitat in Canada across North America and converge in central Mexico about 4,000 kilometers away!

October

Most of the monarchs will remain in diapause condition until the following spring, when they begin to mate in the overwintering colonies.

November

Migratory adults arrive to overwintering sites in central Mexico, around November where they remain till March. The Monarch's arrival is an epic phenomenon that coincides with Día de los Muertos (Day of the Dead), which is observed in Mexican culture between November 1st and 2nd.

Overwintering

December

Monarchs migrate south spend the winter there because they cannot survive the freezing temperatures in the northern parts of their range.
The mega Generation of Monarchs migrate to the mountains of central Mexico, or the Pacific Coast to overwinter in areas that are warm enough for them to spend the winter without freezing, but cool enough for them to not use all their energy when there isn't much food to eat.

January

This mega Generation of Monarchs remains in 'reproductive diapause' when overwintering.

February

In Spring, the mega Generation will start reproducing land lay the eggs that will become the new Generation 1 that will be able to begin a new journey north in March.

Milkweed

Monarchs that overwinter can survive up to 9 months. In contrast, breeding adult monarchs in the spring and summer months need plentiful nectar plants to fuel their reproduction, and usually live only about 1 month.
